About the Project

Where Creativity Meets
Sustainability

This project combines creativity, sustainability, engineering principles, and hands-on design thinking to build something truly meaningful — a working pickup truck toy called the Cargo Cruiser, made entirely from recycled materials.

Every component was chosen deliberately, every design decision tested and refined as a team. The result is a push-back pickup truck that not only entertains but teaches the science of stored energy and motion.

The Challenge

Problem Identified.
Pivot Made.

Real design rarely goes according to plan. Here is the challenge we faced — and how we adapted.

🔄 Our Design Pivot

We originally planned to build a Toyota Prado — a full SUV design with a closed cabin and detailed body panels. We made significant progress cutting and shaping the plasterboard structure. However, midway through the building phase, the plasterboard broke and could not be repaired.

Rather than giving up, our team made a key decision: pivot to a new design that worked better with our remaining materials. We chose the pickup truck format — the Cargo Cruiser — which required less rigid framing and took advantage of our already-cut pieces for the open cargo bed.

① Planned a Prado → ② Plasterboard broke → ③ Team pivoted → ④ Cargo Cruiser born
⚠️

The Wider Problem

Many children do not have access to fun and affordable toys, and large quantities of waste materials like cardboard and plasterboard are discarded daily. This creates both an environmental problem and a gap in hands-on educational play for children.

💡

Our Solution

We designed the Cargo Cruiser — a push-back pickup truck built from recycled and repurposed materials. The pivot from Prado to pickup truck taught us that great design is not about perfection; it is about adaptability, resourcefulness, and teamwork.

Design Brief

What We Set Out to Build

Our toy is a push-back pickup truck called the Cargo Cruiser, designed for children aged 5–12 years. It helps users learn about force, movement, and elastic energy through play. The truck is built from recycled and repurposed materials — cardboard for the main structure, chopsticks as axles, cardboard disc wheels for wheels, rubber bands as the power mechanism, and hot glue for bonding — to reduce cost and environmental waste. It is important because it supports science learning while encouraging creativity, resilience, and environmental responsibility.

How It Works

The Push-Back
Mechanism

Simple physics. Powerful results. Here is the science behind the Cargo Cruiser.

01
👋

Push Backward

The Cargo Cruiser is pushed backward along a surface, stretching the rubber band threaded through the chopstick axle.

02
🌀

Energy Stored

As the rubber band stretches, elastic potential energy is stored. The further you push, the more energy is loaded.

03
🖐️

Release!

When released, the stretched rubber band snaps back instantly, converting stored elastic energy into kinetic energy.

04
🛻

Cruiser Moves!

The released energy spins the chopstick axles and cardboard wheels, propelling the Cargo Cruiser forward.

This demonstrates a core physics principle — elastic potential energy converts to kinetic energy. The rubber band stores and releases energy the same way a slingshot does, making the Cargo Cruiser a hands-on introduction to real engineering concepts.

Components

Materials Used

Every material was chosen deliberately — cheap, accessible, and kind to the planet.

📦
STRUCTURE

Cardboard

Main body and cab framing. Lightweight, easy to cut and shape from recycled packaging.

🧱
ORIGINAL MATERIAL

Plasterboard

Used in the original Prado design attempt. The break led to our pivot to the Cargo Cruiser concept.

🥢
AXLES

Chopsticks

Strong, straight, and perfectly sized to serve as wheel axles running through the cardboard body.

🔴
MECHANISM

Rubber Bands

The power source. Stretch to store elastic energy, then release it to drive the Cruiser forward.

🔫
BONDING

Hot Glue Gun

Fast-setting, strong adhesive used to bond all parts securely — the backbone of our assembly process.

Why these materials? They are inexpensive, easy to source, environmentally friendly, and reusable. Even the broken plasterboard became part of our learning — it forced us to adapt and ultimately led to a stronger, more creative design.

Design Process

From Idea to
Cargo Cruiser

Our journey was not linear — we planned one thing and built another. Here is the full story.

🔍

Step 01

Research

We investigated push-back rubber-band mechanisms, available recycled materials, and design references for our chosen car model.

Step 02

Brainstorming

After exploring several options, our team agreed on building a Toyota Prado — a sturdy, recognisable SUV with strong visual appeal.

💡
✏️

Step 03

Sketching & 3D Modelling

We created hand sketches and 3D digital models of the Prado, planning every panel, window, and dimension of the car.

Step 04

Planning & Material Prep

We wrote a full materials list, assigned roles, and prepared the plasterboard panels for cutting and shaping the Prado body.

📋
💥

Step 05 — Setback

The Plasterboard Broke

Midway through building the Prado body, the plasterboard cracked and could not be repaired. This was a major setback that forced us to rethink our entire approach.

Step 06 — Pivot

Decision: Cargo Cruiser

Our team discussed options and collectively decided to pivot to a pickup truck design. The open cargo bed design worked perfectly with our remaining cardboard materials.

🔄
🔨

Step 07

Building the Cargo Cruiser

We cut and shaped cardboard for the new design, inserted chopstick axles, attached cardboard wheels, threaded rubber bands, and used the hot glue gun to assemble everything.

Step 08

Testing

The first test did not work as expected. We identified and documented the problems to guide our improvements.

🧪
🔁

Step 09

Improvements

We re-tensioned rubber bands, realigned chopstick axles, reinforced joints with additional hot glue, and corrected wheel alignment through precise measurement.

Step 10

Final Product

After multiple iterations, our team completed a fully functional push-back Cargo Cruiser — tested, refined, and ready for exhibition.

Results

Testing &
Evaluation

Every prototype is only as good as the testing that follows it.

❌ First Test — Did Not Work

Our first test on a smooth surface did not go as expected. The Cargo Cruiser barely moved and the rubber-band mechanism failed to engage reliably, with energy not transferring effectively to the wheels.

⚠️ Challenges Identified

The chopstick axles were not perfectly centred, causing the cardboard wheels to wobble. The rubber bands were also too loose and slipped off the axle under tension rather than driving the wheels forward.

🔧 Improvements Made

We re-tensioned and repositioned the rubber bands, re-centred the axles with measurements, and applied extra hot glue to lock the cardboard disc wheels firmly as wheels. The body was reinforced to prevent structural flexing.

✅ Final Result

After our improvements, the Cargo Cruiser moved smoothly and steadily forward across the test surface. The push-back mechanism worked reliably across multiple trials, consistently storing and releasing energy. This confirmed that failure is not the end — it is an essential step in the design process. Each failed test gave us the data we needed to build something better.

Responsible Design

Safe &
Sustainable

🌱

Sustainability

The Cargo Cruiser is built almost entirely from recycled and repurposed materials. Even the broken plasterboard was not wasted — the experience taught us valuable lessons about material properties and adaptive design. By choosing cardboard, chopsticks, rubber bands, and cardboard disc wheels, we actively reduced waste and proved that creative thinking is the most powerful resource of all.

🛡️

Safety

All cardboard edges were folded and sealed to eliminate sharp points. Hot glue bonds ensure no parts detach during play. The rubber-band mechanism is fully enclosed within the body. The design was reviewed with the target age group (5–12 years) in mind — no sharp edges, no small loose parts, no choking hazards.

Final Reflection

What We
Learned

Through this project, we learned how to design, build, test, and improve a working toy as a team using recycled materials. We followed the complete design process — from research and brainstorming to sketching, 3D modelling, building, testing, and iterating.

We learned how the push-back rubber-band mechanism stores elastic potential energy and converts it into kinetic motion, helping us understand real scientific concepts — force, energy transformation, and Newton's laws — through direct, hands-on experience.

The biggest challenge we faced was not the failed test — it was the plasterboard breaking midway through our original Prado design. We had to stop, reassess, and completely change direction as a team. This taught us the most important lesson: resilience and adaptability are as important as technical skill in any design project.

If we had more time, we would strengthen the cardboard body, improve the exterior finish, add painted details, and optimise the rubber-band tension to increase the Cargo Cruiser's travel distance on release.
